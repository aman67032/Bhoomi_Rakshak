Environmental Protection Laws

Understanding the legal framework for mining and environmental protection in India

Key Legislative Framework

India has established a comprehensive legal framework to regulate mining activities and protect the environment

1957

Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act

The foundational legislation that governs mining operations in India. It establishes rules for granting mining leases, permits, and licenses, while providing a framework for state control over mining operations.

Key Provisions:

  • Requires mining lease for extraction of major minerals
  • Enables state governments to make rules to prevent illegal mining
  • Establishes penalties for mining without proper authorization
  • Provides framework for sustainable mining practices
1972

Wildlife Protection Act

While not specific to mining, this act prevents mining activities in protected wildlife sanctuaries and national parks, creating safeguards for biodiversity-rich areas.

Key Provisions:

  • Prohibits mining within national parks and wildlife sanctuaries
  • Requires Wildlife Board clearance for activities within 10km of protected areas
  • Establishes severe penalties for habitat destruction
1980

Forest Conservation Act

This legislation has significant implications for mining as it restricts the use of forest land for non-forest purposes, including mining, without prior approval from the central government.

Key Provisions:

  • Prohibits use of forest land for mining without central government approval
  • Requires compensatory afforestation for diverted forest land
  • Establishes process for environmental impact assessment
  • Provides for payment of Net Present Value (NPV) for forest land
1986

Environment Protection Act

Often called the "umbrella legislation" for environmental protection, this act empowers the government to set standards for environmental quality, emission limits, and procedures for handling hazardous substances.

Key Provisions:

  • Enables setting of environmental standards for all industries
  • Provides framework for Environmental Impact Assessment
  • Grants authority to issue directions for closure of polluting activities
  • Establishes penalties including imprisonment for environmental violations
1995

National Environmental Tribunal Act

Established tribunals for effective and expeditious disposal of cases arising from accidents occurring while handling hazardous substances, including those related to mining operations.

2010

National Green Tribunal Act

Created a specialized body to handle environmental disputes, including those related to illegal mining. The NGT has been instrumental in addressing environmental violations by mining companies.

Key Provisions:

  • Establishes dedicated tribunal for environmental cases
  • Provides for application of "polluter pays" principle
  • Enables orders for restoration of environment
  • Allows NGT to exercise powers of a civil court
2015

Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Amendment Act

This significant amendment strengthened provisions to deal with illegal mining by increasing penalties and establishing special courts for mining-related offenses.

  • Increases penalties for illegal mining up to â‚¹5 lakh per hectare
  • Establishes special courts for speedy trial of mining offenses
  • Creates District Mineral Foundation for affected communities
  • Implements transparent auction process for mine allocations

Implementation Challenges

Despite comprehensive legislation, enforcement remains a significant challenge

Fragmented Jurisdiction

Mining governance is divided between central and state authorities, often leading to confusion and gaps in oversight. The division of regulatory powers between multiple agencies creates coordination challenges.

Potential Solution:

Establishing a unified mining regulatory authority with representation from both central and state governments.

Limited Monitoring Capacity

Government agencies lack adequate personnel and technology for effective monitoring of vast mining areas. Many states have less than one inspector per 100 square kilometers of mining territory.

Potential Solution:

Leveraging satellite monitoring, drone surveillance, and community-based monitoring systems.

Delayed Justice

Legal proceedings for mining violations often take years to resolve, reducing their deterrent effect. Despite creation of special courts, case backlogs remain significant.

Potential Solution:

Strengthening the National Green Tribunal with additional benches and resources for faster case resolution.

Corruption

Widespread corruption in regulatory agencies undermines enforcement of mining laws. Several mining scandals have revealed deep connections between mining operators and officials.

Potential Solution:

Implementing transparent electronic monitoring systems and robust whistleblower protection mechanisms.

Landmark Judgments

Courts have played a crucial role in shaping environmental jurisprudence related to mining

Common Cause vs. Union of India (2017)

Supreme Court of India

In this landmark case concerning illegal mining in Odisha, the Supreme Court:

  • Directed recovery of 100% compensation from mining companies operating without environmental clearances
  • Established the principle that all mineral extraction without proper authorization must compensate the state
  • Reinforced that natural resources belong to the people and must be protected for intergenerational equity
Impact:

Led to over â‚¹17,000 crore in penalties for illegal mining and established stronger precedent for environmental compensation.

Goa Foundation vs. Union of India (2014)

Supreme Court of India

This case challenged illegal iron ore mining in Goa, leading to these significant outcomes:

  • Suspended all mining operations in Goa until proper environmental clearances were obtained
  • Established the critical principle of intergenerational equity in mineral resource management
  • Created a permanent fund from mining proceeds for sustainable development
Impact:

Introduced the concept of a "cap" on mining based on sustainable development principles, fundamentally changing how mining operations are regulated.

Samaj Parivartana Samudaya vs. State of Karnataka (2013)

Supreme Court of India

Addressing the Bellary mining scandal, this judgment:

  • Cancelled 49 mining leases due to severe environmental violations
  • Mandated environmental restoration of mining-affected areas
  • Directed implementation of Reclamation and Rehabilitation plans
Impact:

Established a comprehensive system for categorizing mines based on violation severity and created a framework for environmental rehabilitation of mining areas.
