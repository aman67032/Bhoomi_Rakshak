About Illegal Mining

Understanding the threat to our environment and communities

Illegal mining refers to mining activities that are conducted without proper authorization, in violation of laws and regulations, or beyond the permitted limits. It poses severe threats to our environment, economy, and society.

Bhoomi Rakshak is a citizen-led initiative to raise awareness about illegal mining practices and empower communities to take action against this environmental crime. We work with government agencies, environmental organizations, and local communities to identify and report illegal mining activities.

Our mission is to protect India's natural resources from unsustainable extraction and ensure that all mining activities are conducted in compliance with environmental and social safeguards.

Environmental Impact

The far-reaching consequences of illegal mining on our ecosystem

Illegal mining operations disrupt ecosystems, pollute water sources, and lead to irreversible environmental damage. Beyond the visible destruction, there are numerous long-term effects that impact biodiversity, human health, and climate stability.

Deforestation

Loss of forest cover and biodiversity due to unchecked mining activities. An estimated 170,000 hectares of forest are destroyed annually due to illegal mining in India.

  • Habitat fragmentation affecting wildlife corridors
  • Loss of medicinal plants and forest resources
  • Increased carbon emissions from forest clearing

Water Pollution

Contamination of water sources with heavy metals and toxic chemicals, affecting drinking water quality and aquatic life in surrounding communities.

  • Mercury and cyanide contamination from gold mining
  • Acid mine drainage affecting groundwater
  • Sedimentation of rivers and streams

Soil Erosion

Degradation of land leading to loss of fertile soil and landslides. Illegal mining removes natural vegetation that anchors soil and prevents erosion.

  • Increased vulnerability to landslides
  • Reduced agricultural productivity
  • Formation of unstable land structures

Air Pollution

Dust and particulate matter causing respiratory diseases. Mining operations release harmful particles that can travel long distances, affecting communities far from mining sites.

  • Silica dust causing silicosis in miners
  • Increased rates of asthma in nearby communities
  • Release of greenhouse gases from equipment

Legal Framework

Key National Laws Against Illegal Mining

  • Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957 - Regulates mining sector and prohibits mining without license
  • Environment Protection Act, 1986 - Requires environmental clearance for mining projects
  • Forest Conservation Act, 1980 - Prohibits mining in forest areas without central government approval
  • Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 - Protects wildlife habitats from mining activities
  • Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974 - Prevents water pollution from mining

State-Level Mining Regulations

Each state has additional regulations governing mining activities. Some key provisions include:

  • Requirement of state mining permits and clearances
  • Zoning restrictions for mining activities
  • Royalty payment requirements
  • Local community consultation mandates
  • Environmental restoration bonds

Check with your state's mining and geology department for specific regulations.

Penalties for Illegal Mining

  • Imprisonment - Up to 5 years for illegal mining under MMDR Act
  • Fines - Up to ₹5 lakh per hectare of illegally mined area
  • Seizure - Of equipment and minerals obtained illegally
  • Environmental Compensation - Additional fines for ecological damage
  • Blacklisting - Companies involved may be barred from future mining leases

Legal Framework Against Illegal Mining

Key Legislation

Multiple laws govern mining activities in India, creating a comprehensive legal framework:

  • Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957 - The primary legislation regulating mining activities
  • Environmental Protection Act, 1986 - Sets environmental standards and clearance requirements
  • Forest Conservation Act, 1980 - Restricts forest land use for non-forest purposes
  • Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 - Protects wildlife habitats from exploitation
  • Water Act, 1974 and Air Act, 1981 - Prevent pollution from mining operations

Enforcement Agencies

Multiple government bodies are involved in enforcing mining regulations:

  • Ministry of Mines - Central policy making and oversight
  • Indian Bureau of Mines - Technical regulations and inspections
  • State Mining Departments - State-level enforcement and monitoring
  • National Green Tribunal - Environmental justice and dispute resolution
  • Directorate of Mines Safety - Safety regulations in mining operations

How to Identify Illegal Mining Operations

Warning Signs of Illegal Mining

Being able to identify illegal mining operations is crucial for community monitoring and reporting. Here are key indicators:

Suspicious Timing

Operations conducted primarily at night or during early morning hours to avoid detection.

Unusual Transportation

Unmarked trucks, overloaded vehicles, or transportation during odd hours without proper documentation.

Location Red Flags

Mining activities near protected forests, wildlife sanctuaries, tribal lands, or watershed areas.

Water Discoloration

Unusual coloration or muddiness in nearby water bodies, indicating potential contamination from mining waste.

Lack of Safety Measures

Absence of proper safety equipment, signage, or protective gear for workers at mining sites.

Child Labor

Presence of children or teenagers working at extraction sites or processing facilities.

Community Advocacy Guide

Community Mobilization

Effective advocacy begins with organizing communities affected by illegal mining:

  1. Document Local Impact

    Collect evidence of environmental damage, health issues, and socioeconomic effects on the community.

  2. Form Watchdog Committees

    Establish community monitoring groups to regularly patrol vulnerable areas and record activities.

  3. Build Alliances

    Connect with environmental NGOs, legal aid organizations, and media outlets to amplify your voice.

  4. Engage Local Authorities

    Establish regular communication channels with district officials, police, and forest departments.

Legal Advocacy Tools

Using legal mechanisms can be a powerful approach to combating illegal mining:

  • Right to Information (RTI) - File RTI applications to obtain information about mining permits and clearances
  • Public Interest Litigation (PIL) - Consider filing PILs in High Courts or the Supreme Court
  • Complaints to NGT - The National Green Tribunal accepts direct complaints from affected parties
  • Media Engagement - Partner with investigative journalists to expose illegal operations
  • Social Media Campaigns - Use digital platforms to create awareness and pressure authorities
